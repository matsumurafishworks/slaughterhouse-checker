Our position

Every consumer deserves
to know how their food was killed.

This tool is not anti-religion. It is pro-transparency and pro-animal welfare. It exists because UK law currently allows the method of slaughter to be hidden from consumers — and we think that is wrong.

The animal welfare case

UK law requires that all animals be stunned before slaughter — rendered unconscious so they cannot feel pain during killing. This is considered the gold standard of humane slaughter by the British Veterinary Association, the RSPCA, and the Farm Animal Welfare Council.

There is one legal exemption: religious slaughter. Under the Welfare of Animals at the Time of Killing (England) Regulations 2015 (WATOK), animals may be killed without prior stunning when slaughtered by a Muslim or Jewish slaughterman for the food of Muslims or Jews respectively.

Scientific evidence — including a 2012 report by the European Food Safety Authority — indicates that non-stun slaughter (cutting the throat of a fully conscious animal) can result in a period of significant pain and distress before unconsciousness, lasting from several seconds up to over a minute in cattle. This tool focusses on that specific welfare concern.

What methods are covered

This tool covers all forms of religious slaughter, not just Halal:

  • Halal — non-stun dhabiha: Certified by the Halal Monitoring Committee (HMC). Animals are not stunned. This is the method associated with the primary welfare concern.
  • Halal — stunned dhabiha: Certified by the Halal Food Authority (HFA) and others. Animals are stunned before cutting. The majority of UK halal production (≈88%) uses this method.
  • Kosher / Shechita: Jewish religious slaughter is always non-stun. Certified by Shechita UK. There is no Kosher method that permits pre-stunning.

By covering all three, this tool is relevant to anyone concerned about non-stun slaughter — regardless of the religious tradition behind it.

The transparency gap

There is currently no legal requirement to label meat according to the method of slaughter. This means:

  • You can buy chicken in a supermarket with no indication of whether it was stunned or not before killing.
  • Non-stun meat routinely enters the general food supply unlabelled — appearing in school dinners, hospital catering, restaurants and takeaways.
  • The FSA has refused, via multiple Freedom of Information requests, to publish which specific abattoirs use non-stun methods.
  • The Government consulted on slaughter method labelling in 2021 but has not legislated.

This tool cannot fully solve that problem — but it gives you more information than you had before, drawn from the public data that does exist.

What we are not saying

This is not a campaign against any religion. Religious communities have the legal right to practise their dietary laws, and we respect that right.

Our argument is simple: that right does not override a consumer's right to know what they are eating. Both things can be true simultaneously. Mandatory slaughter method labelling — as exists in several European countries — would allow religious consumers and welfare-conscious consumers alike to make informed choices without restricting anyone's practices.

This tool is for:

  • Animal welfare advocates who object to non-stun slaughter on ethical grounds.
  • Muslim consumers who want to verify the specific halal method used.
  • Jewish consumers who want to verify kosher certification.
  • Non-religious consumers who simply want to know what they are eating.
  • Parents trying to understand what is served in their child's school.

Scotland and Northern Ireland use separate registers (FSS and DAERA respectively) and are not yet covered by this tool.

What you can do

  • Write to your MP demanding mandatory slaughter method labelling on all UK meat.
  • Support the RSPCA — their campaign calls for compulsory labelling of meat from non-stunned animals.
  • Ask your supermarket what their slaughter policy is and whether they stock non-stun meat.
  • Ask your child's school what slaughter method is used in their catering contract.
  • Share this tool — the more people who use it, the greater the pressure for proper legislation.
